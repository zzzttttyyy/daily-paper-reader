# Video-Rate Streaming Stylization on a Vision-Aware MLLM-Conditioned Edit Diffusion: Asymmetric Batched Inference on a Distilled UNet + MLLM Text Encoder 

Yoshiyuki Ootani, Independent Researcher 

Abstract —Aggressive distillation of the diffusion U-Net inverts the per-frame bottleneck of real-time text-to-image pipelines: once the denoiser is a 4-step or 1-step distilled student, the text encoder becomes the critical path. This inversion is most acute in vision-aware edit diffusion, where the encoder is a multimodal large language model (MLLM). We study the case of a 0.39 Bdistilled edit U-Net paired with a 2.13 B MLLM text encoder (Qwen3-VL) and present a streaming pipeline targeted at this regime built around three engineering mechanisms: asymmetric side-stream / main-stream CUDA pipelining with batched text-encoder amortisation (and optional static-prompt caching), a compile-friendly ControlNet-LLLite reformulation that folds the entire U-Net + adapter stack into a single fused graph, and a periodic conditioning-refresh schedule with a hook subset that amortises the per-frame conditioning cost. On a single consumer RTX 3090 Ti at 512 ×512 the pipeline sustains 27 .4 FPS over a 480-frame run at batch size B=8 and 29 .6 FPS at B=16 , with end-to-end p50 latency of ≈0.5 and ≈1.0 s respectively; the same operating point measures 54 .9 FPS on RTX 4090 and 74 .1 FPS on RTX 5090. We report video-rate streaming throughput rather than interactive low latency, and locate our numbers against same-stack StreamDiffusion re-runs as systems context, not as a benchmark superiority claim. For the trained oil-painting style, the released temporal adapter generalises within in-clip noise to 19 unused DAVIS-2017 sequences and 15 non-DAVIS clips from seven sources; prompt-level generalisation to unseen style families is bounded and reported separately. 

Index Terms —Real-time video processing, streaming infer-ence, diffusion models, multimodal large language models, GPU pipelining, video stylization, temporal consistency, edge inference.

We refer to this pattern as the small-distilled-denoiser plus heavy-MLLM-encoder (SDD-MTE) regime: a ≤1 B distilled diffusion denoiser running under torch.compile or Ten-sorRT, paired with a ≥2 B vision-aware multimodal language model that jointly attends to a source image and an instruction string. The three engineering mechanisms reported in this paper are designed for this regime; we instantiate them on the smallest currently documented configuration of the class and would expect them to transfer to other SDD-MTE pair-ings, though we evaluate only DreamLite-mobile+Qwen3-VL empirically. The most acute case of this inversion arises in vision-aware edit-mode diffusion: models that consume both a source image and a textual instruction in order to produce an instruction-conditioned re-render. This class has converged on multimodal large language model (MLLM) text encoders that jointly process image and text tokens. DreamLite-mobile [1] is representative — a 0.39 B parameter U-Net, distilled to 4 steps, conditioned on Qwen3-VL-2B [2] (2.13 B parameters) which jointly attends to a 256 × 256 source patch and an instruction string. The text encoder is roughly 5× larger than the denoiser by parameter count, and it carries an intrinsic CPU–GPU synchronisation point inside its rotary position-embedding code that prevents simple single-stream dispatch from overlapping it with U-Net compute. The temporal-consistency adapters that emerged for SD-class denoisers, in particular ControlNet-LLLite [3], make this regime harder rather than easier. Their forward path uses Python branches and tensor-identity tests that break 

torch.compile ’s graph capture, so a naive LLLite-augmented U-Net runs ∼3.5 × slower than the base U-Net even at the same arithmetic intensity. Wide-range MLLM TE outputs (std = 37 , max = 1160 in our profiling) also overflow FP16 attention, so the TensorRT path that StreamDiffusion-class systems [4] rely on for headline throughput is unavailable here. This paper is a systems study of how to drive a small dis-tilled edit-U-Net + MLLM-TE pipeline at video-rate streaming throughput on a single consumer GPU. A. Streaming and Real-Time Diffusion Video Pipelines 

StreamDiffusion [4] establishes the modern reference point for real-time text-conditioned image diffusion: batched-timestep dispatch, residual classifier-free guidance, stochas-tic similarity filtering, and TensorRT compilation together reach 91 FPS on SD-Turbo and 38 FPS on Kohaku v2.1 +

LCM-LoRA at 512 × 512 on an RTX 4090. StreamV2V [5] extends the framework to video-to-video by maintaining a cross-frame feature bank; StreamDiffusionV2 [6] adds SLO-aware batching and a sink-token–guided rolling KV cache for multi-GPU video-diffusion backbones; StreamDiT [7] and MotionStream [8] target streaming text-to-video generation on dedicated DiT backbones; RTR-DiT [9] and Live2Diff [10] pursue causal real-time video translation with autoregressive DiT or uni-directional 3D-attention bases respectively. Stream-ing Video Diffusion [11] formalises the online-editing setting with a video-token cache; Denoising Reuse [12] amortises noise-prediction cost across frames by reusing inter-frame motion structure. ParaDiGMS [13], DistriFusion [14], and PipeFusion [15] parallelise the denoising trajectory across timesteps or GPUs for offline throughput. A recent line of caching / reuse work on the encoder side is directly relevant: VLCache [16] reuses VLM encoder state across repeated multimodal inputs, Skip-Vision [17] accelerates VLM infer-ence via adaptive visual-token skipping, and InfiniPot-V [18] compresses KV cache for streaming video understanding under memory bounds. Adaptive caching has also emerged on the denoiser side: AdaCache [19] adapts cache cadence to motion in diffusion transformers, and Elastic-Cache [20] formalises when/where to refresh in diffusion LLMs. We adopt a deliberately simple periodic-refresh policy and discuss adaptive alternatives in §IV-C and §VI. Our setting differs from all of the above in two ways: a vision-aware MLLM text encoder makes the per-frame critical path the encoder rather than the denoiser, and a single consumer GPU forbids the multi-GPU patch parallelism that the offline systems assume. B. Vision-Aware and MLLM-Conditioned Generation 

A separate strand pairs diffusion bases with multimodal language models for instruction-conditioned editing or genera-tion. GPT4Video [21] couples a unified MLLM with diffusion decoders for instruction-followed multimodal generation; Mo-Trans [22] customises motion via text-driven video diffusion guidance; unified MLLM-edit systems such as BAGEL [23] (7 B active) and OmniGen2 [24] (4 B diff. + 3 B VLM) place a full MLLM in the conditioning path. DreamLite-mobile [1] compresses this class with a 0.39 B distilled U-Net and a 

2.13 B Qwen3-VL [2] encoder. Among documented vision-aware distilled edit models, this pairing is the smallest com-bination that retains instruction-following at video-stylization quality; the asymmetry it creates between a fast denoiser and a heavy encoder is exactly the regime this paper targets. 

C. Temporal Consistency and Adapter-Based Conditioning 

Cross-frame attention and 3D convolution define the heavy-weight route to temporal consistency (AnimateDiff [25], Stable Video Diffusion [26]). Zero-shot routes rely on op-tical flow or keyframe re-rendering (Rerender-A-Video [27], FlowVid [28]); both are offline. Within TCSVT-adjacent venues, TCVE [29] imposes temporal-consistent video editing via image-diffusion bases with a dedicated motion module; spatio-temporal energy-guided diffusion [30] unifies zero-shot synthesis and editing with an explicit energy prior; Uni-VST [31] targets training-free localised video style transfer; TVG [32] formalises a training-free transition-video genera-tor; FluencyVE [33] replaces full temporal attention with a Mamba state-space mixer plus bypass attention for editing efficiency. ControlNet [34] and ControlNet-LLLite [3] provide the adapter mechanism we build on: LLLite uses per-attention zero-initialised residuals ( ∼13 M parameters at the default rank) gated by a per-block CNN encoder of a conditioning image. D. Distillation and Step Reduction 

Latent Consistency Models [35], adversarial distilla-tion [36], and progressive adversarial distillation [37] reduce inference step count by retraining. We do not retrain in this paper. Instead we show empirically (§IV-B) that the 4-step distilled DreamLite-mobile base degrades gracefully to K=1

A. Notation and Setup 

DreamLite-mobile [1] pairs a 0.39 B U-Net (TAESDXL VAE; FlowMatchEuler scheduler [38]) with Qwen3-VL [2] as the text encoder; text-to-image ( GENERATE ) and image-edit (EDIT ) modes share weights. EDIT concatenates the noisy latent with an encoded reference-image latent along the spatial-width axis, and the TE prompt template injects a ⟨image_pad ⟩ visual token processed by Qwen3-VL’s vision tower. The released checkpoint is 4-step distilled. ControlNet-LLLite [3] attaches 108 adapter modules to the LLL ITE -eligible Linear layers in U-Net attention blocks (ATTN 1.{q, k, v } and ATTN 2.q ); each module contains a per-block CNN that encodes a conditioning image, a zero-initialised residual, and a δ added to its host Linear’s out-put. We train this adapter with the Farneb¨ ack-warped [39] previous decoded frame as the conditioning input and the post-hoc-blended ( α=0 .85 ) base output as the teacher target. All experiments use Python 3.10.0, PyTorch 2.6.0 +cu124, Triton 3.2 (Windows port), on a single RTX 3090 Ti unless noted otherwise. 

B. Asymmetric Side-Stream / Main-Stream Pipelining 

The reference implementation runs a GENERATE -mode en-code followed by a 4-step denoise on a single Python thread. We restructure it into four cumulative steps. 

a) torch.compile of the U-Net: We compile only the U-Net (reduce-overhead , fullgraph=False ,

dynamic=False ). At fixed 512 ×512 shapes the compiled U-Net is 3.1× faster than eager. 

b) CUDA-stream pipelining of TE and VAE-encode: 

The EDIT -mode TE output depends on both the source image and the prompt and in the base operating mode must be recomputed every batch. Its ∼200 ms latency at B=1 can however be hidden behind the next frame’s denoise. We launch TE and VAE-encode for batch n+1 on a side CUDA stream while the default stream runs batch n’s denoise and VAE-decode; synchronisation uses a single CUDA event, so the two streams contend only at memory bandwidth. The TE additionally carries an intrinsic .item() synchronisation point inside its rotary position-embedding code; placing the encoder on its own stream lets this stall overlap U-Net compute on the main stream rather than serialising into the per-frame critical path. 

c) Zero-retraining K=1 inference: Running the released 4-step distilled weights at K=1 (a single σ=1 → 0 flow-matching update) preserves recognisable stylisation at the same prompt with εw within 5% of the K=2 trajectory’s; LPIPS to the K=4 teacher rises to ≈0.40 (Table II). We trade this controlled fidelity loss for a 2× throughput gain; this is an inference-time operating point, not a new distillation. 

d) Asymmetric batched dispatch with TE caching: At fixed K=1 , Table V (§IV-B) shows that the Qwen3-VL TE amortises substantially better with batch than the compiled U-Net: TE wall grows from 125 ms at B=1 to 153 ms at 

B=4 and 233 ms at B=8 (sub-linearity s=0 .319 and 0.226 ), while the compiled U-Net grows from 23 ms to 74 ms and 

142 ms ( s=0 .806 and 0.770 ). We exploit this asymmetry by buffering B=8 frames before each pipeline call; per-frame TE drops from 125 to ∼30 ms while per-frame U-Net cost rises only marginally. Latency cost is B−1=7 frames of buffering (∼230 ms at 30 FPS source). 

e) Optional TE caching modes for static-prompt streams: 

When the user instruction is fixed across the stream and the source visual content changes slowly, we addition-ally expose two opt-in switches that further amortise the TE: (i) te_batch_one encodes only one representative 

(image , prompt ) pair in the batch and broadcasts its key/value tensors to the remaining B−1 frames, shrinking the multi-modal sequence by B×; (ii) te_refresh_every = NTE 

caches the broadcast TE output across NTE consecutive batches so it is recomputed once per B · NTE source frames. C. Compile-Friendly LLLite Reformulation 

Attaching the temporal LLLite adapter on top of the compiled U-Net breaks torch.compile [40]: the re-leased LLLite forward contains five Python branches (a multiplier=0 short-circuit, a cond_emb is None 

guard, dtype/device mismatch checks, a sequence-length fallthrough, and a dropout-only-in-training path), and the cached COND EMB tensor is reassigned (not in-place updated) inside set_cond_image , changing tensor identity and forc-ing Dynamo to recompile on every frame. We refactor the adapter for inference as follows: (i) register 

cond_emb as a fixed-size buffer of shape (Bmax , N, d cond )

with persistent=False (old checkpoints load un-changed); set_cond_image writes in-place, preserving ten-sor identity; (ii) register the multiplier as a buffer too, written via set_multiplier_tensor ; updates do not invalidate the graph; (iii) expose a separate forward_inference 

method with no Python branches — dropout, dtype, and shape-fallback paths are pruned and the host Linear’s δ is unconditionally added; (iv) apply the adapter in-place via 

apply_lllite(...,inference_mode=True) , which patches forward_inference into the host Linear’s 

forward attribute. After this refactor an LLLite-augmented U-Net at B=8 , K=1 averages 10 .12 ± 0.11 FPS across 10 DAVIS sequences versus 2.13 ± 0.03 FPS for the eager LLLite reference — a 4.75 × speed-up with no change to the trained weights. 

D. Periodic Conditioning Refresh and Hook Subset 

Even compile-friendly, the 108 LLLite hooks each invoke a small CONDITIONING 1 CNN every time set_cond_image 

is called. At B=8 this CNN forward is 4× a single image’s cost and dominates the LLLite path’s wall time. 

a) Cond-refresh interval: Because consecutive frames share most scene content, the warped previous output — the LLLite cond input — changes slowly. We rebuild cond_emb 

every N -th batch and reuse the previous embedding in be-tween. With B=8 and N =8 a refresh fires once per 64 frames ( ∼2 s at 30 FPS ); the cached embedding remains a good approximation for intermediate batches and empirically does 

not degrade reference fidelity or warping error versus per-batch refresh (§IV-C). The Farneb¨ ack flow itself is threaded and async-kicked during prefetch_batch so it overlaps the next side-stream slot rather than stalling the wall. 

b) Hook subset: Reducing the active hook count is the second axis. The 108-hook attachment splits into 38 on down_blocks , 16 on mid_block , and 54 on 

up_blocks on the DreamLite-mobile U-Net. Restricting to 

down_blocks -only (38 hooks, ≈35% of the full set) cuts conditioning-encoder work by the same factor; trained weights for dropped hooks are silently ignored at load time. The restriction recovers stylisation sharpness from Sobel 1.88 (all-108) to 4.74 (close to the no-LLLite baseline 5.10 ) and LPIPS-to-teacher from 0.54 to 0.38 (Table II). We adopt it as the default operating point. 

c) Caveat on εw in the smoothing regime: A LLLite configuration with too much attribute authority — e.g., 108 hooks at multiplier 1.0 — pushes outputs toward a desaturated “smooth purple” attractor. The Sobel / HF-FFT / LPIPS triplet in Table II catches this immediately (108-hook rows: Sobel

18 .34 ) because the output is nearly static. We therefore recommend always pairing warping error with at least one spatial high-frequency probe in this regime; the LCM-LoRA distillation case study reported in §V is the direct application of this rule. IV. E XPERIMENTS

N EGATIVE RESULTS AND CASE STUDIES 

We summarise five engineering dead ends and one distilla-tion case study; each ruled out a tempting direction and shaped the final recipe. 

a) Naive previous-latent init mixing: Mixing the previous output latent into the noisy init at frame t (a standard SD streaming trick [4]) degenerates within ∼10 frames into a self-reinforcing single-colour attractor on the 4-step distilled flow-matching scheduler. The 4-step schedule lacks the integration steps to pull the trajectory back on-manifold once the init is off-distribution. εw falsely drops (the output is near-static); reference fidelity Lref  

> 1

catches the failure. 

b) Cond-image manipulation in distilled edit models: 

Replacing the right half of the spatial-concat EDIT input with a flow-warped previous output, a learned T2I keyframe, or a blend all break: the distilled U-Net trained with the cond-image matching the input distribution drifts to an abstract red/orange blur when fed a stylised intermediate or a halluci-nated keyframe. Cond-side conditioning is not a free temporal channel. 

c) Pure flow-warped speculation: Speculatively predict-ing frame t+1 by warping frame t’s latent and skipping the U-Net unless flow magnitude crosses a threshold, in the spirit of speculative decoding [46], reaches the noise floor of the base flicker without a temporally-consistent base ( ∼30 vs. input flicker ∼28). εw is constructively low ( ∼11) because the output is by construction a flow-warp of the previous frame; the metric is invariant under exactly that warp. We use this as motivation for cond-refresh: amortising the LLLite cost via 

N > 1 is much cheaper than a learned skip mechanism. 

d) TensorRT FP16 with wide-range MLLM TE out-puts: The pure-FP16 TRT 10.16 U-Net engine produces visibly broken outputs in pipeline: Qwen3-VL TE out-puts have unusual range (std 37 , min −854 , max 1160 ), and the encoder_hid_proj into the U-Net attention dimension overflows FP16 attention/softmax kernels se-lected by TRT. Standard remedies — per-layer FP32 hints on NORMALIZATION , SOFTMAX , and MATRIX MULTIPLY ;

OBEY_PRECISION_CONSTRAINTS ; FP32 ONNX →FP16 engine — do not recover accuracy. A pure-FP32 engine is numerically equivalent to PyTorch (cos-sim 0.999996 ) but at 23 .7 FPS , slower than the PyTorch +compile path. An LLLite-baked TRT export (38 hooks’ cond_emb exposed as separate engine bindings) is structurally correct (FP32 cos-sim 0.999999 , FP16 at 2.14 × PyTorch U-Net wall) but blocked by the same FP16 attention overflow (cos-sim drops to 0.77 on real Q3-VL embeddings). FP8-capable hardware (H100, Blackwell) with explicit per-tensor attention scaling is a plausible direction we have not verified. 

e) TE template-token pruning absorbed by side-stream slack: Removing the 80-token Qwen3-VL system prompt from the chat template cuts TE wall by 13% (262 → 229 ms at B=8 ) in isolation, but does not move end-to-end fps (30 .09 → 30 .10 ): the saving is fully absorbed by side-stream slack because the main stream sets the wall. Quality regresses slightly ( εw 18 .4 → 19 .9), consistent with the system prompt being load-bearing for instruction-tuned behaviour. 

Lesson: once side-stream pipelining is in place, the per-frame bottleneck migrates to the slower stream and improvements to the faster stream are invisible without also accelerating the slower one. 10

The remaining gap is a combination of (i) K=1 inference under-sampling the diffusion trajectory of a 4-step distilled base, (ii) v-prediction MSE being dominated by low-frequency error, and (iii) the choice of per-frame teacher not transferring temporal structure that the LLLite path obtains explicitly via the warped previous output. Methodological takeaway: on this pipeline, εw alone is not a sufficient temporal-consistency metric; pair it with a spatial-fidelity probe (Sobel, HF-FFT, or LPIPS) before declaring a smoothness gain. We presented a systems study of video-rate streaming-throughput stylisation on a vision-aware MLLM-conditioned distilled edit-diffusion stack — an operating point of 27 –

30 FPS sustained with 0.5–1.0 s end-to-end p50 latency, not an interactive low-latency one. The inversion of the per-frame bottleneck once the U-Net is distilled — the MLLM text encoder becomes the critical path — makes the stan-dard streaming-V2V design centre (denoiser acceleration) the wrong starting point. We instead show that three engineering mechanisms — asymmetric side-stream / main-stream pipelin-ing with batched TE amortisation and optional static-prompt caching, a compile-friendly LLLite reformulation, and a pe-riodic cond-refresh schedule with a hook subset — together sustain 27 –30 FPS (B ∈ { 8, 16 }; 32 .7 FPS short-window peak) at 512 ×512 on a single RTX 3090 Ti, 54 .9 FPS on RTX 4090, and 74 .1 FPS on RTX 5090, with the released temporal adapter transferring within in-clip noise to 19 unused DAVIS se-quences and 15 non-DAVIS clips from 7 sources. Limitations. The in-domain quality numbers use a v3 adapter trained on the same 10 DAVIS sequences they are evaluated on; the held-out splits address this on the video axis but the held-out-prompt experiments show adapter quality degrades gracefully outside the training-prompt manifold. The 1-step inference recipe is empirical on DreamLite-mobile only. 

Future work. Four directions are natural: an LLLite-aware TRT FP8 path on H100/Blackwell hardware (the LLLite-baked export reported here is already structurally correct); a learned 1-step distillation specialised for this pipeline with a perceptual +adversarial loss (our LCM-LoRA case study suggests v-prediction MSE is the wrong objective); a lower-resolution ( 256 2-native) LLLite retrain to probe whether the asymmetric-batched-dispatch recipe still applies once the TE / U-Net cost ratio shifts further toward the denoiser, as it typi-cally does on resource-constrained hardware; and a motion-adaptive cond-refresh schedule that replaces the fixed N -batch policy with a flow-magnitude or salience-driven trigger, following the adaptive-cache lineage of [19], [20], [47].
