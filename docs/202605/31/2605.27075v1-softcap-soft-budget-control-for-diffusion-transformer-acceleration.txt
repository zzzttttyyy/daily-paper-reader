Abstract 

Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) achieve strong visual quality, but their iterative denoising pro-cess requires many costly Transformer eval-uations. Training-free acceleration methods reduce this cost by caching, forecasting, or ver-ifying intermediate features, yet the runtime decision of when to execute a Full step is of-ten driven by fixed schedules or hand-tuned thresholds. We propose SoftCap , a training-free control layer for cache-based DiT infer-ence. SoftCap couples a Trajectory Drift Ob-server, which estimates local cache risk from lightweight hidden-state statistics, with a Soft-Budget PI Controller, which adjusts the Full-triggering threshold from realized compute rel-ative to a fixed reference profile. The budget is a soft ceiling: it shapes the threshold but does not require a run to spend a prescribed number of Full evaluations. On FLUX.1-dev, SoftCap improves over SpeCa at a comparable middle-compute operating point, raising Im-ageReward from 0.967 to 0.981 and reducing LPIPS-Full from 0.518 to 0.498 at nearly iden-tical FLOPs, while target-sweep diagnostics show the intended soft-ceiling behavior as the budget is relaxed. 

1 Introduction 

Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) have become a cen-tral backbone for high-fidelity visual generation, but their inference cost remains high. A single sample requires many denoising evaluations, and each evaluation runs a large Transformer network over high-dimensional latent tokens. This cost is especially visible in recent text-to-image and video systems, where generation latency and serv-ing throughput are constrained not only by the num-ber of sampling steps but also by the per-step cost of attention and feed-forward blocks. Reducing this cost without retraining the base model is there-fore a practical requirement for deploying large DiTs. Training-free feature caching has emerged as a practical way to reduce the per-step cost of diffusion inference. Rather than executing all Transformer blocks at every denoising step, cache-based methods reuse or approximate intermedi-ate features. Representative examples include DeepCache-style feature reuse and TaylorSeer-style trajectory forecasting (Ma et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025c); speculative feature caching further studies when a predicted feature should be accepted (Liu et al., 2025d). Together, these methods show that diffusion trajectories contain substantial tem-poral redundancy and have advanced the design of cache engines and feature predictors. The remaining question is when a runtime sys-tem should trust a cached approximation. Many Full/Cache policies still rely on static schedules, fixed thresholds, or manually selected acceptance rules. Such open-loop policies miss an important asymmetry: the value of a Full evaluation depends on both local approximation risk and the amount of computation already spent along the current sam-ple trajectory. When the run is close to its compute ceiling, a moderate-risk cached approximation may be acceptable; when the run has spent less compute than expected, a high-risk step may justify a Full evaluation. Conversely, if the trajectory remains stable, executing additional Full steps only to meet a nominal target may bring little marginal benefit. DiT acceleration therefore needs a policy layer that regulates Full evaluations under a compute ceiling, in addition to a cache engine that approximates features. We propose SoftCap, a training-free soft-budget control framework for accelerating DiTs. Soft-Cap treats Full/Cache selection as a feedback-controlled decision rather than a fixed caching schedule. It combines a Trajectory Drift Observer (TDO), which estimates the risk of continuing cached approximation from lightweight drift cues, with a Soft-Budget PI Controller, which adapts the

A final Risk-Gated Full/Cache Decision com-pares the observer risk with the controller threshold and selects either a Full evaluation or the underly-ing cache engine. The design deliberately separates local trajectory risk from global compute state: the observer asks whether the current approximation looks risky, while the controller asks how strict the risk gate should be under the current soft budget. A key property of SoftCap is that its budget pa-rameter is a soft computational ceiling , not a re-quired spending target. The controller can make Full evaluations easier or harder to trigger, but it does not force the system to spend computation when the observed trajectory risk remains low. This behavior is useful for deployment: a user-specified compute limit should shape latency without requir-ing unnecessary model evaluations. We evaluate SoftCap on FLUX.1-dev with a 200-prompt text-to-image benchmark. Under a consis-tent generated-vs-full evaluation protocol, SoftCap provides a competitive quality-efficiency tradeoff against representative caching, forecasting, and speculative baselines. Target-sweep diagnostics further support the soft-ceiling interpretation: re-laxing the budget raises the allowed compute, but the realized number of Full evaluations saturates when the observer does not identify enough high-risk steps. Detailed metrics and matched-compute comparisons are provided in the experimental sec-tion. Our contributions are: • We formulate DiT Full/Cache scheduling as a soft-budget feedback control problem and in-troduce SoftCap, a training-free control layer for feature caching. • We design a Trajectory Drift Observer that es-timates caching risk from low-cost magnitude, direction, anchor-deviation, and temporal-volatility cues. • We introduce a Soft-Budget PI Controller that adapts the Full-triggering threshold from re-alized compute relative to a fixed reference budget profile. • We provide FLUX.1-dev evidence showing that SoftCap yields a competitive quality-efficiency tradeoff and that its budget param-eter behaves as a soft computational ceiling rather than a required number of Full evalua-tions. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Efficient Diffusion and DiT Inference 

Diffusion models generate samples through iter-ative denoising, which makes inference more ex-pensive than single-pass generative models. DiTs further increase the per-step cost because their de-noising networks rely on Transformer blocks with attention and feed-forward computation over many latent tokens (Peebles and Xie, 2023). Existing acceleration methods reduce either the number of denoising evaluations or the cost of each evaluation. Step-reduction methods include improved numeri-cal solvers, distillation, consistency-style modeling, and consistency transfer for multimodal diffusion systems (Qiu et al., 2025b), but they may require a different sampler or additional training. SoftCap fo-cuses on the complementary setting: the sampling schedule and base model are kept intact, and the per-step network cost is reduced by training-free feature caching. 

2.2 Feature Caching and Forecasting 

Feature caching exploits temporal redundancy across nearby denoising steps. Early cache-based methods reuse intermediate activations from pre-vious steps to skip parts of the denoising network (Ma et al., 2024; Selvaraju et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025a). This strategy is attractive because it can be applied as an inference-time wrapper, but direct reuse becomes less reliable when the cache hori-zon grows. Forecasting-based methods address this issue by predicting the current feature from histor-ical states. TaylorSeer models feature trajectories with finite differences and Taylor expansion (Liu et al., 2025c); PrediT uses linear multistep fore-casting with a corrector for high-dynamics regions (Cui et al., 2026); FoCa interprets hidden-feature evolution through an ODE lens and combines fore-casting with calibration (Zheng et al., 2026). Other work decomposes the feature space itself. FreqCa separates low- and high-frequency feature dynam-ics (Liu et al., 2025b), while SVD-Cache uses a subspace-aware decomposition to stabilize princi-pal and residual components (Chen et al., 2026). A related branch explicitly compensates cache-induced approximation error: Error-Optimized Cache introduces prior-knowledge-based correc-tion for reused modules, and Gradient-Optimized Cache propagates cached feature differences to re-duce positional drift in skipped computations (Qiu et al., 2025c,a). 2.3 Adaptive Caching and Verification Policies 

Several recent methods improve caching by mak-ing the decision more adaptive. ToCa observes that tokens have different sensitivity to cached ap-proximation and applies token-wise feature caching (Zou et al., 2024). TAP uses a low-cost probe to select token-level predictors (Zhu et al., 2026). ClusCa clusters spatial tokens and computes repre-sentative tokens within each cluster (Zheng et al., 2025). RFC estimates output-feature changes from input-output relations and uses relational cache scheduling to trigger recomputation when expected changes are large (Son et al., 2026). Hybrid-grained caching further shows that cache granu-larity can differ across model components, such as block-level and prompt-level reuse in controllable generation (Liu et al., 2026). These works show that uniform caching policies are often insufficient because approximation risk varies across tokens, layers, samples, denoising stages, and model com-ponents. Speculative verification offers another perspec-tive on the Full/Cache decision. SpeCa, for example, predicts intermediate features with a lightweight draft mechanism and verifies whether the drafted features should be accepted (Liu et al., 2025d). This line of work studies the reliability of a proposed approximation. SoftCap uses a different abstraction: it does not introduce a draft-and-verify pipeline, but places a feedback policy around a cache engine. Its observer summarizes runtime tra-jectory state through drift cues, and its controller adjusts the Full-triggering threshold according to realized compute. This shifts the policy question from only “is this approximation reliable?” to “how strict should the risk gate be under a soft compute ceiling?” 

2.4 Budget-Aware Compute Allocation 

The most relevant distinction of SoftCap is its treat-ment of compute allocation. Existing caching meth-ods often report results at different acceleration ra-tios, but the runtime policy is rarely formulated as a closed-loop budget controller. SoftCap maintains a soft budget ceiling, a fixed reference progress profile, and the realized number of Full evalua-tions. The discrepancy between the reference and realized compute controls the risk threshold, while the Trajectory Drift Observer supplies the local approximation-risk signal. This decoupling makes the role of each component explicit: the observer estimates trajectory-dependent risk, the controller regulates how strict the decision should be under the current compute state, and the final gate con-verts risk crossings into Full evaluations. SoftCap therefore acts as a control layer for cache-based DiT acceleration rather than a replacement for ex-isting cache engines or feature predictors. 3 Method 

3.1 Preliminaries Diffusion Transformer inference. A diffusion model generates a sample by iteratively denoising a noisy latent. At each denoising step, a neural de-noiser predicts an update direction and the sampler advances the latent to the next noise level. In DiTs, the denoiser is implemented as a stack of Trans-former blocks over latent tokens. Let hlt denote the hidden feature at layer or module l and denoising step t. A standard DiT inference pass evaluates all relevant blocks at every step, which makes the total cost scale with both the number of denoising steps and the per-step Transformer computation. 

Feature caching. Training-free caching reduces this cost by reusing or approximating hidden fea-tures at selected steps. A cache engine stores an anchor feature from a recent Full evaluation and uses it to approximate later features. If the approx-imation is accepted, expensive downstream block computation can be skipped; if it is rejected, the model performs a Full evaluation and refreshes the cache state. SoftCap operates in this cached-inference setting. It is cache-engine agnostic in principle, and we instantiate it with a Taylor-based cache engine in our experiments.

4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate SoftCap on FLUX.1-dev text-to-image generation. The main benchmark contains 200 prompts, and all accelerated outputs are compared with images generated by the Full FLUX baseline under the same prompts and 1024 × 1024 resolu-tion. SoftCap is instantiated with the Taylor cache engine described in Section 3. Unless otherwise noted, the main comparison uses the base weighted TDO score, while the optional positive-increment switch is evaluated separately. We compare against representative training-free acceleration baselines, including SpeCa (Liu et al., 2025d), TaylorSeer (Liu et al., 2025c), and FORA (Selvaraju et al., 2024). The primary effi-ciency evidence in our comparison is the measured average FLOPs in tera-FLOPs (TF), with speedup computed relative to the Full FLUX baseline. For quality, we report ImageReward and CLIPScore as semantic preference and text-image alignment metrics. We also report internal Full-referenced fidelity metrics, including FID-Full, PSNR-Full, SSIM-Full, and LPIPS-Full. These Full-referenced metrics compare each method’s generated images against the Full FLUX outputs on the same prompts. They are therefore useful for same-protocol rela-tive comparison, but should not be interpreted as dataset-FID or as universal perceptual scores.

4.3 Positive-Increment Switch 

Table 2 isolates the optional positive-increment term in the Trajectory Drift Observer. The switch is not a separate method; it only changes how the observer responds to upward changes in the base risk score. At the middle and lower compute set-tings, enabling the switch improves several Full-referenced fidelity metrics and ImageReward, with small or moderate FLOPs changes. At the higher-FLOPs setting, the base score is preferable on most semantic and efficiency metrics. We therefore re-port the base TDO score in the main comparison and treat the positive increment as an optional cali-bration knob for settings where stronger response to abrupt risk increases is desired. 

4.4 Soft-Ceiling Diagnostic 

The main table evaluates quality and efficiency, but it does not by itself show whether Ncap behaves as a soft ceiling. We therefore run a five-prompt target-sweep diagnostic in which the SoftCap bud-get ceiling is varied while the rest of the policy is kept fixed. This diagnostic is smaller than the main benchmark and should be read as mechanism evidence rather than as a standalone quality bench-mark. Table 3 shows the result. Increasing Ncap from 8 to 40 raises the allowed compute envelope by a factor of five, but the realized Full count increases only from 10 to 13. This is the intended behav-ior. The controller changes the threshold, but Full evaluations still require trajectory-risk crossings. When the observer does not identify enough high-risk steps, the run can remain below the ceiling instead of spending extra computation simply be-cause the ceiling is available. In this diagnostic, the risk crossings are converted into Full evalua-tions, so the plateau is not caused by a downstream eligibility failure. Additional platform diagnostics support this in-terpretation. Risk crossings are converted into Full evaluations in the checked runs, and neither forced Full paths nor max-skip guards explain the satu-ration pattern. The plateau therefore appears to come from limited natural risk crossings, not from a downstream eligibility bottleneck. This distinc-tion matters: SoftCap is designed to regulate the strictness of the risk gate, not to force the realized compute to hit a prescribed target.

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, We presented SoftCap, a training-free soft-budget control framework for cache-based DiT acceleration. Instead of treating Full/Cache scheduling as a fixed schedule or a manually se-lected threshold, SoftCap separates two decisions that are often conflated: whether the current cached trajectory appears risky, and how strict the Full-triggering gate should be under the realized com-pute state. In future work, we will explore adaptive reference accumulation curves to achieve more precise alignment with the computational budget, thereby maximizing genera-tion quality under a given computational quota.
