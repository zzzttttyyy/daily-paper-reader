## Abstract 

We introduce Sticky Jump Diffusions (SJDs), continuous-time Markov processes on 

Rd whose discrete anchors are token embeddings. In forward time, anchors release their mass at a hazard rate and the released mass diffuses in the continuous ambient space; time reversal couples a score-driven SDE with a sticky jump kernel whose rate and destination are fixed by flux balance with the forward law. We estimate the score and the per-anchor reverse hazards from a single denoising classifier via 

Denoising Hazard Matching , the hazard analogue of denoising score matching, with simulation-free cross-entropy training. SJD recovers masked diffusion, continuous diffusion, and hybrid diffusion as limits. Its reversal explains features that each family treats as given: the mask of masked diffusion carries no evidence about the source token because the unsticking kernel of every anchor collapses to the same absorbing point; the terminal projection of continuous diffusion is required due to the absence of atoms in its forward marginal, without which flux balance yields no reverse jumps; and the update rules of hybrid diffusion (commit rate, destination, and drift) all follow from flux balance rather than from separate design. Beyond these limits, the unsticking kernel becomes a design space: a cross-position blending corrupts each position toward a blend of its neighbors’ clean values or embeddings, turning dependency structure such as spatial locality or a constraint graph into an inductive bias of the corruption itself, and improves over the identity-kernel hybrid on CIFAR-10, Text8, and Sudoku. Our code is available at https: //github.com/PascalJD/sticky-jump-diffusions .

Masked diffusion is discrete but gradient-free; continuous diffusion is gradient-based but requires a terminal projection; SJD couples ambient gradients with sticky jumps onto anchors. absorbing token, and the per-position factorization erases graded information about how close the corruption is to a candidate token [Austin et al., 2021, Sahoo et al., 2024, Shi et al., 2024, Lou et al., 2024]. Continuous diffusion operates on token embeddings and uses standard score-based learning, but its terminal state lies off the discrete support and must be mapped back by a projection step external to the dynamics [Li et al., 2022, Dieleman et al., 2022, Chen et al., 2023]. Recent hybrid diffusion pairs the two: a masked chain decides which positions are committed, and a continuous latent carries graded information for the masked ones [Zheng et al., 2026, Pynadath et al., 2026, Zhou et al., 2026]. A review of these three families is given in Section B. We define Sticky Jump Diffusions (SJDs), a class of continuous-time Markov processes on a continuous state space X = Rd with a finite or countable set of anchors A ⊂ Rd identified with token embeddings. All three families above arise as limits of SJDs. In forward time, data start on anchors; an anchor releases its mass at a hazard rate ⃗ λt into the continuous region XA, where the trajectory diffuses by a standard SDE. The marginal pt splits into a continuous density on XA and atoms on A, generalizing the classical sticky-boundary diffusions of a single absorbing point on the half-line [Feller, 1952, 1954, Itô and McKean, 1963] to anchor sets of vocabulary size living in Rd. Figure 1 visually compares SJD with masked and continuous diffusion. Time reversal of an SJD produces a generative process with two coupled mechanisms: a score-driven SDE on XA and a sticky jump kernel that commits mass to anchors. The per-anchor reverse intensities are fixed by flux balance [Conforti and Léonard, 2022], so the choice of where to commit and at what rate is determined by the forward law. Token commitment is therefore a reverse-time jump event of the stochastic process, with a rate and a destination that the dynamics specify, rather than a hand-designed schedule applied at sampling time. To turn the reverse-time identity into a usable generative model, we introduce Denoising Hazard Matching (DHM), the hazard analogue of denoising score matching [Vincent, 2011]: the reverse hazard equals the conditional expectation of a forward-known target under the SJD corruption. Combined with a denoising classifier Pθ , DHM yields both the score and the per-anchor reverse intensities from the same network, by an analytic reweighting of Pθ that is Rao–Blackwellized and needs no separate hazard head. Training is plain cross-entropy on SJD-corrupted pairs and is simulation-free under standard conditions on the forward dynamics. The same construction places existing model classes inside SJD, and each limit inherits an explanation from the reversal. In masked diffusion, the unsticking kernel collapses to the absorbing token, and commitment is forced to be time-only. In hybrid diffusion, the kernel matches the forward perturbation kernel: commit rate, destination, and drift follow from flux balance. In continuous diffusion, there is no sticking jump: without atoms in the forward marginal, there are no reverse jumps, so a terminal projection outside the dynamics is needed. The intermediate regime is the one we study empirically: the unsticking kernel there carries a cross-position blending toward a weighted combination of neighbors’ clean values or embeddings, which improves over the identity-kernel hybrid on CIFAR-10, Text8, and Sudoku. 2Contributions.

## 4 Connections to Existing Diffusions 

The SJD construction of Section 2 unifies several existing model classes by varying three forward-process levers: the unsticking kernel ra, the forward hazard ⃗ λt, and, in the VP-matched family of Theorem 2.2, the blending matrix W and off-anchor variance ratio η. Different limits of these quantities recover absorbing-state discrete diffusion, pure continuous diffusion, and recently-proposed hybrid diffusion. We make these correspondences precise below. 

4.1 Masked Diffusion as a Degenerate-Kernel Limit 

Masked diffusion models (MDMs) [Austin et al., 2021, Sahoo et al., 2024, Shi et al., 2024] are the SJD whose off-anchor region collapses to a single absorbing point. Identify A = {e(v) : v ∈ V} 

with the embedded vocabulary, fix a mask location m / ∈ A , and set

[2026] structural: because every anchor unsticks to the same point, the conditional law pac  

> t

Correspondingly, the spatial factor of the DHM target (15) reduces to the constant 1/(1 − αt): the commit rate is time-only, and the allocation can be informed only from context, through the classifier (Section 4.5). 

4.2 Continuous Diffusion as the No-Jump Limit 

Setting ⃗ λt ≡ 0 shuts off jumps and reduces (2) to a standard SDE; Theorem 2.6 reduces to the classical score-based time-reversal of Song et al. [2021]. For discrete data, three external mechanisms appear in the literature. (i) Decoder: train a continuous diffusion in latent space and push samples through a separately-trained decoder [Li et al., 2022, Dieleman et al., 2022, Lovelace et al., 2023]. (ii) Quantization: Bit Diffusion [Chen et al., 2023] thresholds the terminal state. (iii) Doob’s h-transform: Liu et al. [2023] bias the SDE by the gradient of a singular log-density so its terminal law lands on a discrete set. A recent wave of embedding-and one-hot-space models closes the gap to discrete diffusion [Yang et al., 2026, Hu et al., 2026, Chen et al., 2026, Lee et al., 2026]; in all of them, discreteness is still restored by a step outside the dynamics, whether a decoder, a readout, or an argmax. SJD instead enforces discreteness through the dynamics: anchors are absorbing in reverse time as a direct consequence of (7).

4.4 Sticky Boundaries: A Classical Antecedent 

SJD’s theoretical antecedent is the classical theory of sticky-boundary diffusions [Feller, 1952, 1954, Itô and McKean, 1963]. A one-dimensional sticky diffusion holds at the boundary for an exponential time and re-enters the interior according to a fixed jump-out measure; its law splits as an interior density plus a boundary atom [Bou-Rabee and Holmes-Cerfon, 2020]. SJD lifts this model to a multi-anchor, high-dimensional setting: A replaces the boundary point, ⃗ λt replaces the sticky coefficient, and Ra replaces the jump-out measure, with Theorem 2.3 as the corresponding mass split. 84.5 Commitment Schedules and Token Ordering

5.3 Solving Sudokus 

Sudoku probes the blending axis W when the governing structure is a hard combinatorial constraint graph. The task is to complete a 9 × 9 grid so that every row, column, and 3 × 3 box is a permutation of {1, . . . , 9}, which requires the model to honor board-level constraints rather than local statistics. We encode those constraints directly into the unsticking kernel. The full construction is given in Section E.3. We then ask whether this constraint-aware corruption helps the model learn the board’s logic. Table 1 isolates three effects of W . Across 6 seeds CADD is bimodal: several runs collapse to near-chance performance. SJD never collapses. Moreover, by board takeoff (the training step at which a seed first resolves complete boards) SJD takes off roughly 4× sooner; even the CADD seeds that avoid collapse begin solving boards far later. Finally, SJD’s mean board accuracy exceeds CADD’s and the autoregressive solver granted the oracle solving order. 

## 6 Conclusion 

Sticky Jump Diffusions place masked, continuous, and hybrid diffusion inside a single jump-diffusion process, fit by plain cross-entropy via Denoising Hazard Matching. Because the reverse process is the genuine time reversal of the forward law, the hazard rate and destination token are fixed by flux balance, not a hand-tuned schedule. The same forward law turns the reverse hazard into a conditional expectation that a single denoising classifier estimates, with no second network. Where previous hybrids hold the unsticking kernel fixed, our framework treats it as an explicit design space. We empirically study the cross-position axis of that space, centering each position’s corruption on a weighted combination of its neighbors’ embeddings, a coupling that a per-position kernel cannot express. We hope this process-level account makes masked, continuous, and hybrid diffusion easier to extend: new corruptions, schedules, and commitment rules can be specified as forward dynamics and obtained in reverse for free. 10 References

Absorbing-state masked diffusion [Austin et al., 2021, Sahoo et al., 2024, Shi et al., 2024] operates on a discrete state space V ∪ { m} with a special mask symbol m. A classifier Pθ (v | xt, t ) is trained by cross-entropy on masked positions and substitutes for the posterior at sampling time. The per-position factorization is exact in the forward law and couples positions only through the classifier’s context input; once a position commits, the only signal carried about its proximity to alternative tokens is whatever the classifier reconstructs from the surviving context.

Lscore is conditional denoising score matching; because the induced score of Section D.3 is exact at the cross-entropy optimum, we train no score head and optimize LCE + LRB alone. Every w-dependence is reparametrizable: unlike GenMD4, whose discrete forward pass forces a REINFORCE estimator [Shi et al., 2024, App. I.2], the continuous off-anchor state yields an exact ∂w from one backward pass. In the degenerate-kernel limit of Section 4.1 the score term vanishes and LCE reduces to the GenMD4 negative ELBO under 

αt,a ↔ Sa(t), so the objective generalizes GenMD4’s.
