## 1 Introduction 

Diffusion models [ 1, 2, 3 ] have emerged as a leading paradigm in generative modeling, achieving state-of-the-art results across image synthesis [ 4, 5], video generation [ 6 ], audio synthesis [ 7], and molecular conformation generation [ 8]. Their operating principle is conceptually elegant: a forward stochastic differential equation (SDE) progressively corrupts data with noise, and a learned reverse process‚ÄĒparameterized by the score function‚ÄĒremoves this noise to reconstruct realistic samples. This framework, unified by Song et al. [ 3], admits two equivalent reverse-time descriptions: a stochastic SDE and a deterministic probability flow ODE that preserves the same marginal distributions at every timestep. For conditional generation, classifier-free guidance (CFG) [ 9 ] has become the de facto standard. Unlike classifier-based guidance [ 4], which requires an auxiliary classifier, CFG blends the score functions of a conditional model pdata and a reference model qdata (typically the unconditional model) through a guidance strength parameter ŌČ:

sŌČt (x) = ŌČ‚ąáx log pt(x) + (1 ‚ąí ŌČ)‚ąáx log qt(x). (1) When ŌČ > 1, the conditional score is extrapolated beyond the reference score, often improving visual quality and prompt alignment but reducing sample diversity and causing supersaturation. Because it requires neither auxiliary networks nor additional training, this simple extrapolation underpins modern text-to-image systems [ 5 , 10 , 11 , 12 ] and has also been adopted in video [6], audio [13], and 3D synthesis [14]. Despite this empirical success, a basic distributional question remains unresolved: what distribution does CFG actually sample from? A common intuition is that CFG samples from the normalized product distribution ňúr0 ‚ąĚ pŌČ

Resolving this question is practically important: an exact characterization can clarify how CFG departs from the na√Įve product picture, explain failure modes such as mode collapse and color oversaturation, and guide the design of principled time-dependent schedules. Existing theoretical studies have provided explanations of failure modes, product-distribution heuristics, stagewise dynamics, correction mechanisms and so on, but they do not directly characterize the sampling distribution induced by the deterministic CFG reverse process. ## 3 Related Work 

3.1 Diffusion Models 

Diffusion models generate samples by learning to reverse a Markovian noising process. The foundational framework was introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al. [ 1] and popularized by Ho et al. [ 2 ] with DDPM. Song et al. [ 3] unified diffusion models and score-based generative modeling through SDEs, connecting denoising diffusion with score matching [ 18 , 19 ]. Deterministic samplers such as DDIM [ 16 ] are closely related to the probability flow ODE, which preserves the same time marginals while enabling faster sampling. Subsequent advances have improved sample quality [ 4 , 20 ], accelerated inference [ 21 , 22 ], and extended diffusion models to latent spaces [ 5, 23 ]. Our analysis builds on the probability flow ODE viewpoint and asks how CFG changes the distribution transported by these deterministic dynamics. 

3.2 Classifier-Free Guidance and Variants 

Classifier-free guidance was proposed by Ho and Salimans [ 9] as an alternative to classifier-based guidance [ 4 ]. By jointly training conditional and unconditional score models, CFG removes the need for a separately trained classifier while retaining strong conditional generation performance. It has become a standard component of large-scale text-to-image systems [5, 10, 11, 12] and has been extended to video [6], audio [13], and 3D synthesis [14]. Several variants have been proposed to address known failure modes. CFG++ [ 24 ] applies guidance only to the denoised estimate while using the unconditional prediction for re-noising, which reduces off-manifold errors and improves invertibility. Dynamic thresholding [ 10 ] adaptively clips and rescales the CFG-predicted clean image at each sampling step, mitigating color oversaturation at high guidance scales. Karras et al. [ 25 ] apply autoguidance using a smaller or less-trained version of the model itself as the reference model. Adaptive projected guidance (APG) [ 26 ] decomposes the CFG update into components parallel and orthogonal to the conditional prediction, identifies the parallel component as a major source of oversaturation, and rescales it to suppress artifacts at high guidance strengths. These methods improve practical behavior, but they do not directly characterize the probability distribution induced by score blending along the reverse trajectory. 

3.3 Theoretical Understanding of CFG 

Despite CFG‚Äôs practical ubiquity, its theoretical understanding remains limited. Bradley and Nakkiran [ 27 ] showed that CFG should not be interpreted as directly sampling a gamma-powered product distribution under either DDPM or DDIM; instead, in the SDE limit, it can be viewed as a predictor-corrector procedure combining conditional denoising with a Langevin-style sharpening step. Pavasovic et al. [ 28 ] studied CFG in high-dimensional settings, showing that it recovers the target conditional distribution in the infinite-dimensional limit, while finite-dimensional corrections produce mean overshoot and variance shrinkage. Jin et al. [ 29 ] developed a theory for CFG under multimodal conditional distributions, showing that guided sampling can proceed through direction shift, mode separation, and concentration stages, thereby motivating time-varying guidance schedules. Skreta et al. [ 30 ] introduced Feynman‚ÄďKac correctors for sampling from product distributions with pretrained diffusion models, using sequential Monte Carlo to correct the CFG heuristic. Gao et al. [ 31 ] analyzed score discrepancies between conditional and unconditional distributions across timesteps, deriving bounds that motivate a time-dependent control function for guidance strength. Our contribution is complementary to these works. Rather than focusing on a particular failure mode, justifying a product-distribution approximation, or adding external correction terms, we derive an exact analytic path-integral representation of the density ňÜrt0 induced by the deterministic guided reverse process. The resulting characteristic formulas characterize the distribution induced by CFG under the probability flow ODE, up to normalization. 

3.4 Guidance Schedule Design 

Varying guidance strength over the sampling trajectory is not new. Several works have observed that the optimal guidance strength may differ between the coarse high-noise phase and the fine low-noise phase of generation. Simple schedules such as linear ramps or step functions have also been explored heuristically [ 10 ]. More recently, time-varying schedules have been proposed: ő≤‚ÄďCFG [ 32 ] uses a beta-distribution-shaped curve to suppress guidance near both endpoints of the trajectory, while interval guidance [ 33 ] applies CFG only within a selected noise-level window, showing that guidance can be harmful at very high noise levels and unnecessary at very low noise levels. These results support the importance of temporal allocation, but the functional forms are not derived from the induced distributional representation itself. 5.1 Motivation: Why Constant Guidance is Suboptimal 

With constant guidance ŌČ(s) ‚Č° ¬ĮŌČ, the schedule-dependent weight reduces to ő≤(s)(¬Į ŌČ ‚ąí 1) . Thus, a nominally constant guidance strength does not produce a constant contribution to the path integral; the effective per-timestep guidance weight is modulated by two orders of magnitude. This imbalance has visible consequences. The high-noise phase primarily determines coarse semantic layout, global structure, and color, whereas the low-noise phase refines details and textures. When constant CFG is combined with a rapidly increasing ő≤(s), guidance is concentrated disproportionately in the high-noise regime. This can encourage repeated coarse layouts and color bias, reducing global diversity and increasing oversaturation. Meanwhile, the low-noise regime receives comparatively little effective guidance, which can leave fine details under-refined. These effects become more pronounced at large guidance strengths because the entire path-integral correction is amplified by ¬ĮŌČ ‚ąí 1.

6.2.5 Minimal NFE to Reach Fixed Quality Targets 

The NFE sweep above compares methods at equal sampling budgets. We now ask the converse question: given a fixed quality target, how many sampling steps does each method require to reach it? We fix ¬ĮŌČ = 23 , a high-guidance regime that is both challenging and informative. Strong guidance accentuates common artifacts such as color over-saturation and amplifies discretization errors, making the effect of NFE easier to observe. At the same time, if these artifacts are controlled, this regime offers the greatest potential for improving prompt alignment and perceptual quality. ## 7 Conclusion 

We studied classifier-free guidance through the deterministic probability flow ODE and derived exact analytic path-integral representations of the induced distribution. The resulting characterization shows that CFG modifies the target distribution through an exponential path-integral correction, whose temporal contribution is governed by ŌČ(t) ‚ąí 1. This perspective explains how guidance accumulates along the sampling trajectory and motivates DG-CFG, a schedule that balances the path-integral contribution across timesteps while accounting for signal strength and low-noise score errors. Experiments on a controlled toy model closely match the theoretical predictions. Large-scale evaluation on Stable Diffusion 1.5 further shows that DG-CFG improves the diversity‚Äďfidelity trade-off across guidance strengths and NFE budgets, with stronger saturation control than constant and heuristic baselines. Fixed-quality comparisons indicate that DG-CFG achieves comparable image quality with fewer sampling steps, and component-wise ablations confirm that each factor in the schedule contributes to its performance. 

Limitations and Future Work. The proposed schedule corrects temporal non-uniformities in the noise schedule, but it does not explicitly optimize the geometric structure of the score discrepancy. Extending the theoretical analysis to flow matching models is a natural direction and may lead to analogous guidance principles for a broader class of generative models.
